COACHING INTERPRETATION — DC

Polisky is the offensive hub — both scorer AND distributor. She had 6 assists vs. USC alone, feeding Arceri, Devir, and Oakey. She initiates from the left, and her passing lanes radiate outward. Disrupting Polisky's passing is the single highest-leverage defensive action. If you shut down her shot, she distributes. If you deny her the ball, the entire offense stalls.

Oakey operates from behind the cage (X position) and feeds the crease. She is the secondary distributor — her connection to Polisky/Arceri is the crease-feed pipeline. Deny the X-to-crease pass and you collapse their inside offense.

Hasselbeck is a finisher, not a creator. She receives feeds from Oakey and Polisky near the crease and converts. She has minimal outgoing connections. Denying her the ball at the crease is more important than pressuring her with the ball.

If you deny Polisky the ball: The offense reorganizes to Oakey from behind and Arceri in isolation. This is a LESS efficient offense — Arceri's unassisted goals are lower percentage than Polisky-orchestrated possessions. Force Stanford into an Arceri-initiates-from-the-right offense, which is more predictable.

GAME PLAN — VIRGINIA vs #2 STANFORD

Tactical blueprint for Feb 28, 2026 · Klöckner Stadium · 12:00 PM ET

2025 HEAD-TO-HEAD: STANFORD 13, VIRGINIA 7 (FEB 22, 2025)

What happened: Virginia jumped out 3-0 in the first 10 minutes (DiNardo 2G, Pederson 1G). Then self-destructed: 10 cards (4 yellow) created free-position and extra-player opportunities. Stanford scored on power plays to spark a 6-0 run. Virginia managed only 3 shots in the entire second half. Case and Polisky each scored 4 goals.

What changes now: Virginia has Finnelle in goal (elite), Brady coordinating defense (structured), and DiNardo/Alaimo/Foster a year more experienced. Stanford lost Brindisi (best defender) and Baldwin. The 3-0 start in 2025 proves Virginia CAN attack this defense. The collapse was 100% discipline, not talent.

OFFENSIVE PLAN vs STANFORD DEFENSE — OC

1. Target Pearson HIGH. Upper corners from inside 8m. DiNardo and A. Foster are the primary shooters in settled situations. Do NOT shoot low-bounce — that's her strength.

2. Attack the Brindisi void. Stanford's best individual defender (All-ACC 2nd Team) graduated. Rose, Johnson, Bellissimo are capable but unproven at this level. Isolate the newer defenders 1v1 with Foster and DiNardo.

3. Run full possessions. Target 50+ seconds per set. Work through Alaimo (2nd in ACC in assists) to find quality looks. The Notre Dame blueprint: 26 shots on 15 allowed, controlling tempo.

4. Conservative clears. Stanford's ride is aggressive — Bouvier and Sommers intercept cross-field passes. Carry the ball, don't throw it across the field. Clemson had multiple turnovers on horizontal passes.

5. Pace target: Sub-20 combined goals. Virginia wins 9-7, 10-8, 11-9. Virginia loses 15-12, 17-11.

DEFENSIVE PLAN vs STANFORD OFFENSE — DC

1. Shadow Polisky with Cruthirds. Face-guard. Deny the ball. Force her to be a passer from outside her comfort zone. Polisky with 6A but 3G (vs. USC) is FAR less dangerous than Polisky with 7G (vs. Vanderbilt).

2. Deny transition. All 6 defenders behind the restraining line BEFORE Stanford's attack sets. If ball turns over, nearest player body-checks carrier to slow the push. 5 seconds of delay kills their transition advantage.

3. Pack middle, force outside shots. Stanford cutters (Devir, Peters-type movement) score through the middle. Collapse inside. Concede low-percentage outside shots over crease finishes.

4. Ride Bouvier's clearing side. She leads in CT and is their primary clearing mid. Send 2 riders at her side to force turnovers. Stanford cleared at 88.9% vs Clemson — below elite. Pressure here creates possessions.

5. Slide timing: Adjacent slide from the crease. Bellissimo is their aggressive low defender — she initiates for Stanford. Virginia should mirror this: adjacent slide, recover to crease, force the extra pass that Stanford turns over.

DRAW CONTROL PLAN — GALICA vs FRIST

This is the game's most important individual matchup. Galica (282+ career DCs) vs Frist (330+ career DCs, 2x All-American). Both are elite. The difference will be made by wing positioning.

Stanford rotates draw takers: Frist, Case, Lawson, Sommers, Bouvier, Arceri. Despite Frist, they lost the draw 9-12 vs Clemson and 6-10 vs Syracuse. Teams with a dominant single specialist CAN win the circle.

Plan: Galica exclusive draw taker. Station the two fastest midfielders on wings. Direct the sweep toward Virginia's strong-side wing. If Stanford rotates Case onto the draw (less polished than Frist), push pace immediately on that possession.

TARGET: Win 55%+ of draws (12+ of ~22 total). Virginia won draws convincingly at Notre Dame. Replicate that here.

⚔ IN-GAME DECISION TREE

IF Virginia wins 4+ of first 6 draws: Push tempo on offense. Exploit Pearson before Stanford's D sets. Build early lead like the 3-0 in 2025.

IF Stanford goes on a 3-0 run: IMMEDIATE timeout. Sub fresh defensive legs. Run full 60-sec possession on next offensive set. Tell Galica to take an extra second on next draw.

IF Polisky has 3+ goals by halftime: Switch to box-and-one. Dedicated face-guard on Polisky with zone behind. Accept that Arceri or Hasselbeck may score but limit Polisky touches to zero.

IF Virginia leads by 2+ entering Q4: Possess the ball. Run 50+ second sets. Force Stanford to foul to get possession back. Every possession eaten = 45+ seconds off clock.

IF Finnelle struggles (2+ soft goals in Q1): Consider Josephson. This is a must-win ACC game. Do not let the goalie question linger into Q2.

IF Stanford loses draws but leads on score: They are winning via transition off turnovers. Tighten ball security. Carry clears, don't pass.
