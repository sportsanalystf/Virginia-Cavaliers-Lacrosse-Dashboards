HOW TO READ THIS CHART

Each dot represents one shot attempt across Notre Dame's 4 games. Gold dots/diamonds = goals scored, gray = missed or saved. The field shows the correct NCAA women's lacrosse markings: the inner circle is the crease (2.6m), the solid arc is the 8-meter, and the dashed arc is the 12-meter fan.

Use the player filter buttons above to isolate individual shooting tendencies. This is the most important pre-game tool for defensive assignment planning.

COACHING INTERPRETATION

Timarky (filter her name above) clusters almost entirely inside the crease with bounce shots. She is a point-blank finisher — if she catches the ball within 3 meters of the cage, she scores at 55%+. Virginia's low defender MUST body her out of this zone before the pass arrives.

Rassas operates from the right side of the 8m arc at mid-range (4-7m). She shoots direct and hard. Force her wider — her conversion drops significantly past the 8m line. Do NOT let her drive inside the arc uncontested.

Maroney (Fr.) is the X-factor — she'll shoot from anywhere through traffic. She scored 3 vs #3 BC from three different zones. She's unpredictable, which makes her dangerous but also less efficient at any single spot.

Zone summary: Crease shots convert at the highest rate (~58%), 8m arc is their volume zone (~48%), and 12m+ shots are low-percentage (~20%). ND's offense is designed to get the ball inside the 8m.

HOW TO READ THIS CHART

COACHING INTERPRETATION

Low zones are ND's money spots: Low-center converts at 70% (14/20) and low-left/low-right at 64%. This is overwhelmingly where Timarky's shovel shots and Rassas's low drives finish. If you see an ND attacker loading up near the crease, the shot is going low.

Mid-tier is their volume zone: Mid-center (57%, 16/28) is where the most shots go. This is the natural center-mass target for most shooters. Mid-left and mid-right are both at 56%.

Top corners are weak: Top-left at 25%, top-right at 29%. ND rarely beats goalkeepers high. This tells us their shooters favor quick-release low shots over placement shots to the upper corners.

For Finnelle: Anticipate low shots. Set your stance low. On crease feeds, cheat down and force the shooter to go top shelf — ND is not comfortable there.

COACHING INTERPRETATION

Patterson's entire low zone is weak — save percentages of 25-29% across the bottom three cells. She struggles to get down quickly, especially on shots to her left (attacker's low-left). This is a freshman positioning issue: she sets too high and can't recover downward.

Mid-center is a volume trap: Only 29% save rate on 14 shots. When Virginia drives to the crease and releases point-blank, Patterson doesn't have the reaction time. She needs time to set — don't give it to her.

Top-left is her best zone (67% save rate). She tracks high-left shots well and positions her body there naturally. Shooting top-left plays into her strength. Avoid it.

In-game adjustment: If Patterson makes 3+ early saves and looks settled, switch to skip shots and off-angle releases to test her footwork laterally. Freshman goalkeepers struggle most with shots that force them to move and reset.

This is a force-directed network graph showing who assists whom on Notre Dame's offense. Each node (circle) is a player — the bigger the node, the more total goals they've scored. Arrows point from the assister to the scorer, and thicker arrows mean that connection happened more frequently across the 4 games.

Players who are central to the network (many arrows in/out) are the offensive hubs. Isolating them defensively disrupts the entire system.

COACHING INTERPRETATION

Maura Irish (Fr, #3) is the offensive hub. She has assist connections to Rassas (2x), Timarky, Maroney, and O'Hare. She is the primary distributor — the ball flows through her on most settled offensive possessions. Disrupting Irish's passing lanes is the single highest-leverage defensive action Virginia can take.

Morrissey (#14) is the secondary distributor — she feeds wide to Rassas, Timarky, Bacigalupo, Maroney, and Conley. She spreads the ball more broadly than Irish, making her harder to completely neutralize but less devastating on any single connection.

The Rassas ↔ Timarky connection is reciprocal. They assist each other — meaning when Virginia doubles one, the other becomes the immediate release valve. Virginia should plan for this: the double team on Rassas must be accompanied by a recovering defender on Timarky, not a gamble.

O'Hare (#5) is a two-way hub. She both feeds and scores. She connects to Irish, Rassas, and Timarky. On defense she disrupts — on offense she's the third passing lane. Don't ignore her in the game plan.

⚠ CRITICAL WARNING FOR COACHING STAFF

Notre Dame is a scoring-run team. This is the #1 behavioral pattern from the first 4 games:

• vs #3 BC: Trailed 9-6 entering Q4. Scored 6 unanswered goals in the final frame — including 2 by Rassas in the first 65 seconds. BC scored ZERO in Q4.

• vs #25 Harvard: Trailed 0-6. Scored 10 of the final 11 goals. Timarky scored 3 in Q2 alone to ignite the comeback.

These are not gradual comebacks — they are explosive 5-8 minute stretches where Notre Dame accelerates tempo and opponents freeze.

RULE: Call timeout after ANY 2-0 Notre Dame run. Do not wait for 3-0. By the time it reaches 4-0, the game dynamic has already shifted and the damage is done.

DEFENSIVE PRIORITY ORDER FOR VIRGINIA

1. Timarky (13G, 54% conv) — The most efficient scorer on the roster. Close-range specialist who converts over half her shots. Must be bodied out of the crease zone before the pass arrives. If she catches the ball within 3m, she scores.

2. Rassas (9G, 43% conv, Tewaaraton Watch) — Power shooter from mid-range. She scored 9 in 4 games but she's the one who sparks the runs — 2 goals in 65 seconds to open Q4 vs BC. Force her to the outside and make her shoot from distance.

3. Irish (5G, 8A = 13pts) — She's the engine, not the finisher. Her 8 assists lead the team. Shutting down her distribution is the highest-leverage defensive action because it disrupts EVERYONE else's scoring.

4. Maroney (7G, 54% conv) — The freshman X-factor. Scored 3 vs #3 BC including the game-tying goal. She shoots through traffic and doesn't have a predictable zone. Respect but don't overcommit to her — she benefits from overreaction to Rassas/Timarky.

5. O'Hare (6G, 2A) — Two-way midfielder who creates both ways. She's the possession you forget about until she scores. Consistent 1-2 goals per game from the midfield.

6. Bacigalupo (6G, 3 vs CMU) — CMU inflated her numbers. Against ranked competition she's a role player with 1G per game. Watch her but don't burn a defensive assignment on her unless she's hot.
